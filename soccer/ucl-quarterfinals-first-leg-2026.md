# Champions League quarterfinals first leg: four games, four away wins, and one absolute mess at Camp Nou

**Date:** 2026-04-09
**Topic:** Soccer / Champions League

---

Four Champions League quarterfinal first legs. All four won by the away team. That hasn't happened in the quarterfinal stage since 2018-19. And the way it happened tells you everything about where European football is right now.

## Barcelona 0-2 Atlético Madrid  - the one that'll be talked about for weeks

This game turned on two minutes of chaos in the 42nd-44th minute, and depending on which fanbase you ask, it was either justice or robbery.

Pau Cubarsí brought down Giuliano Simeone  - Diego's actual son, because football loves a storyline  - on a clean breakaway. Referee Istvan Kovacs pulled out a yellow. VAR stepped in. After review, it was upgraded to a red. Last man, clear goalscoring opportunity, textbook denial. Cubarsí walked.

Julián Álvarez stepped up to take the resulting free kick and curled it into the top corner. 1-0 Atlético. Barcelona down to 10 men at home in the Champions League quarterfinals. Camp Nou went from 90,000 people singing to complete silence in 120 seconds.

Then the second controversy. In the second half, Marc Pubill  - already on a yellow  - handled the ball inside the box. Clear as day on the replay. Flick saw it. Everyone in the stadium saw it. The VAR team? Nothing. No review, no penalty, no second yellow, nothing.

Alexander Sørloth made it 2-0 in the 70th minute and that was that.

Flick's post-match press conference was barely contained fury. He specifically questioned why the VAR  - a German official  - failed to intervene on the Pubill handball after being so eager to intervene on the Cubarsí yellow. The Referee Committee's response made it worse, not better: they said the VAR shouldn't have upgraded Cubarsí's yellow to a red in the first place. So the referee got it right originally, VAR overruled him incorrectly, Barcelona played 50 minutes with 10 men because of it, and the system worked as intended? What?

The thing is, Atlético were the better team regardless. Simeone had a plan and executed it perfectly. Giuliano Simeone was electric on the right. Álvarez was world class. This is the same Atlético side that hadn't won at Camp Nou in 20 years. They didn't just win  - they dominated a 10-man Barça and could've scored three.

But none of that matters in the narrative now. All anyone's talking about is VAR. Again.

## PSG 2-0 Liverpool  - the quiet demolition

While Barcelona and Atlético were producing drama, PSG were producing a masterclass. And Liverpool were producing... nothing.

Désiré Doué scored in the 11th minute  - his shot deflected off Gravenberch and looped over the keeper. It was scrappy. It didn't matter. PSG had the lead and never looked remotely troubled.

The stats are embarrassing for Liverpool. 74% possession for PSG. 18 shots to 3. Zero  - zero  - Liverpool shots on target. They didn't register a single shot in the first half, which hasn't happened to Liverpool in any competition since April 2021 against Real Madrid. They completed 82 passes before halftime, the fewest in any Premier League or Champions League game since December 2007.

Khvicha Kvaratskhelia made it 2-0 in the 66th minute, rounding Kelleher and finishing calmly. His fourth consecutive Champions League knockout goal  - first PSG player to ever do that.

Arne Slot admitted Liverpool were "ripped apart." He's not wrong. This was men against boys in the Parc des Princes. PSG could've won 5 or 6.

And here's the thing nobody's saying out loud: Liverpool have now lost 4-0 to Man City in the FA Cup and 2-0 to PSG in the Champions League in the same week. Mo Salah was benched for the PSG game. The wheels haven't just come off  - the entire car is in a ditch.

## Bayern Munich 2-1 Real Madrid  - Kane is inevitable

This was supposed to be the blockbuster. Real Madrid at home in the Champions League, Mbappé vs everyone, the Bernabéu under the lights. Instead, Bayern walked in and punched first.

Luis Díaz  - yes, the same Luis Díaz who used to play for Liverpool  - gave Bayern the lead late in the first half. Then Harry Kane scored 20 seconds into the second half. Twenty seconds. Madrid hadn't even settled back into shape and Kane had already buried it. His 49th goal of the season.

Mbappé pulled one back, because Mbappé in the Champions League at the Bernabéu is still Mbappé. But 2-1 to Bayern means they go back to Munich with the advantage, and this Bayern side with Kompany at the wheel looks nothing like the team that lost 4-3 on aggregate to Madrid last season.

Kane is having the season of his life and nobody's paying attention because it's happening in Bavaria instead of North London. The man has 49 goals. He might hit 60. And he has a Champions League semifinal to play for.

## Sporting CP 0-1 Arsenal  - ugly, efficient, Arteta

Arsenal went to Lisbon and did what Arsenal do under Arteta: survived, suffered, and found a way. Kai Havertz scored in the 91st minute. Stoppage time winner. Of course.

The game was nothing for 89 minutes. Sporting were organized. Arsenal couldn't break them down. David Raya made a couple of crucial saves. It looked like a 0-0 that everyone would forget by morning.

Then Gabriel Martinelli played a ball through, Havertz got on the end of it, and suddenly Arsenal have a 1-0 lead going into the second leg at the Emirates. Arteta's post-match interview was the most relaxed he's been all season. He knows what a 1-0 away lead means for a team that defends like Arsenal.

## The bigger picture

All four away teams won. Home advantage in European football is dying, and the Champions League quarterfinals just proved it. Camp Nou, the Bernabéu, the Parc des Princes  - none of them could protect their team.

Some of this is tactical. Simeone, Kompany, Luis Enrique  - these are managers who build game plans specifically for hostile environments. Sit deep, absorb, counter. It's not pretty but it works.

But some of it is structural. The new Champions League format means these teams have already played 8 league phase games plus knockout rounds. The squads are stretched. Home crowds don't compensate for tired legs the way they used to.

The second legs are next week. Atlético take their 2-0 lead back to the Metropolitano. PSG host Liverpool again... wait, no. Liverpool host PSG at Anfield. And if there's one ground in Europe where a 2-0 deficit means nothing, it's Anfield. Bayern go home with a 2-1 lead against a Madrid side that has pulled off more Champions League comebacks than anyone. Arsenal protect a 1-0 lead at the Emirates, which is basically what they were born to do.

None of these ties are over. That's the entire point of two-legged European football. But some of them are closer to over than others. Liverpool need a miracle. Barcelona need two goals and a working VAR system. Arsenal need to be Arsenal. And Bayern need to not be Bayern circa 2024.

## My take

The Barcelona-Atlético game is going to define this Champions League round in the history books, and not because of the football. The Referee Committee admitting the VAR upgrade was wrong  - after Barça already played 50 minutes down a man  - is the kind of thing that erodes trust in the entire system. You can't retrospectively acknowledge a mistake that directly caused a 2-0 deficit in a Champions League quarterfinal and then just move on. What's the remedy? What's the point of VAR if it can make the correct decision worse?

But I also think Barça fans need to be honest: they were getting outplayed before the red card. Simeone's Atlético had a plan and it was working. The red card turned a competitive game into an uphill battle, but it didn't turn a Barcelona dominance into a defeat.

Liverpool's week is the more concerning story. 4-0 in the FA Cup, 2-0 in the Champions League, Salah benched. Something is fundamentally broken and Slot knows it. The Anfield second leg will tell us whether this is a dip or a collapse.

And Harry Kane quietly having a 49-goal season while everyone argues about VAR is the most Harry Kane thing that's ever happened.
