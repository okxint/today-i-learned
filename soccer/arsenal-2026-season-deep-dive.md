# Arsenal won the league. Now they want everything.

The Premier League title is confirmed. The UCL final is six days away. Budapest on May 30. This is the season Arsene Wenger spent 22 years building toward and never got to see completed.

**Date:** 2026-05-25
**Topic:** Soccer / Arsenal

---

Twenty-two years is a long time to wait for anything. It's long enough for an entire generation of Arsenal fans to grow up having never seen their team lift a Premier League trophy. Long enough for the jokes to stop being funny. Long enough for "we're building something" to become white noise.

This season, the building is done.

Arsenal won the Premier League title on May 19 when Manchester City dropped points against Bournemouth. They didn't even need to play. The title came to them because they had done everything right over nine months and City, for the first time in years, blinked. Final table: Arsenal 85 points, City 78. Seven points. Not a close race in the end.

And now, six days from now, they face PSG in Budapest for the Champions League. The double is a real thing. Not a fantasy. A real, measurable, achievable thing.

## What this season actually was

People are going to reach for the Invincibles comparison because that's what Arsenal fans do. Resist it. This team is not those teams. The 2003-04 side had Thierry Henry scoring 30 league goals. This Arsenal had Kai Havertz as their top scorer with 9. They went the entire Premier League season without a single player hitting double figures in goals. First time that's happened in 101 years at this club.

Think about that. Arsenal won the Premier League with a collective attacking output that no single striker carried on his back. They did it with structure, with depth, with a defensive record that was borderline obscene.

30 clean sheets in the league. David Raya matched a club record last set by David Seaman in 1993-94. 34 goals conceded all season, the league's best. Arsenal's expected goals against was consistently the lowest in the division. Their shots against per game was the lowest. This was a team that made themselves genuinely hard to score against and then ground out enough wins to make 85 points.

That is not the sexy narrative. But it is the accurate one.

## The Arteta project, finally mature

Mikel Arteta took over in December 2019 when Arsenal were 10th in the table and thoroughly broken. He's been building since then and the criticism has always been the same: beautiful football, great system, but does he actually win things?

This season answered that.

What changed tactically in 2025-26 was the coordination. Arteta has always wanted a high press but in earlier seasons it was disorganised at the seams. The team would press, the press would break, and suddenly there were spaces that shouldn't be there. This season the triggers are sharper. When Arsenal win the ball in the press, the transition is immediate and pointed. When they can't win it high, they drop into a mid-block that's compact enough to suffocate most attacks before they develop.

The 4-3-3 and 4-2-3-1 switching is seamless now. Arsenal in buildup are a wide team, stretching the field to create lanes for midfield runners. Arsenal in the final third go narrow, overloading central areas and forcing defenders into decisions they don't want to make. This is not accidental. Arteta drills this. Every player knows their role within it.

The second-ball dominance is the most underappreciated part. After a contested header, after a corner cleared, after a throw-in scramble, Arsenal win the loose ball at a rate that's remarkable. Declan Rice is central to this. He covers ground that shouldn't be one player's responsibility and keeps doing it for 90 minutes. He does not get enough credit for how much work Arsenal's structure asks of him.

## William Saliba is a generational defender

He is 24 years old. He has been Arsenal's best player for two seasons and the conversation hasn't quite caught up with the reality yet. Saliba is elite. Not Premier League elite, not Arsenal-specific elite. European elite. The combination of pace, reading of the game, aerial dominance and composure in possession is rare at any age. At 24 it's remarkable.

Gabriel alongside him has been excellent too. But Saliba is the anchor. On the nights Arsenal have looked vulnerable it's usually because Saliba has been tested repeatedly and eventually something gets through. The nights they've looked unbeatable it's because Saliba has made everything look straightforward.

Raya has been superb in goal. 19 clean sheets from him personally. His distribution is a genuine weapon in Arteta's build-up system. Most goalkeepers are the endpoint of play, the last resort. Raya is the starting point. Arsenal build through him, not away from him.

## Viktor Gyokeres and what he solved

The one obvious gap in every previous Arsenal season was a reliable, physical centre-forward. Alexandre Lacazette left. Pierre-Emerick Aubameyang left on bad terms. Gabriel Jesus had moments but couldn't stay fit. Eddie Nketiah was asked to do things that his profile wasn't built for.

Gyokeres fixed this. What he brings is not just goals. It's the presence. He holds the ball up. He drags centre-backs out of position. He gives Arsenal a reference point to build around that they simply did not have before. When Gyokeres is on the ball 25 yards from goal with his back to the defender, Arsenal's midfielders run because they know the ball is coming back under control. That confidence changes how the whole team attacks.

His Champions League moments have been defining. The penalty against Atletico in the first leg at the Metropolitano, converting under pressure in a 1-1 draw away from home. The Fulham brace that made the title race look finished. This is a player who delivers in the moments that matter.

The fact that he wasn't the top Premier League scorer tells you something interesting about how Arsenal use him. He's not a penalty-box poacher waiting for chances. He works. He creates. And when he scores, it tends to count.

## The Champions League run nobody expected

Cast your mind back to August. Arsenal had not won the Champions League. Arsenal had been in one final, in 2006, when Lehmann got sent off after 18 minutes and they still nearly won with ten men before Eto'o and Belletti broke their hearts. The club has been in the competition every year since 2023, going deep but not quite deep enough.

This season the draw was kind in some places and brutal in others. Bayern Munich in the league phase was the game everyone circled. Arsenal won 3-1 at the Emirates. That result reframed expectations. You don't beat Bayern 3-1 in the Champions League and still think of yourself as a work in progress.

The semi-final against Atletico was the test of character. Simeone's teams do one thing: they make football miserable. The Metropolitano in the first leg was exactly what you'd expect. Two penalties, no open play goals, a 1-1 draw that left everything open. Gyokeres scored Arsenal's. Julian Alvarez equalised for Atletico. VAR cleared a potential third penalty that would have given Atletico the lead. Arsenal came away with a draw and the knowledge that one goal at the Emirates would be enough.

The second leg was a controlled performance. Bukayo Saka scored in the 44th minute, a rebound after Oblak parried Trossard's shot. Arsenal held the lead for 46 minutes with the kind of organised, disciplined defending that Arteta has made their identity. Atletico couldn't break them. When the final whistle went the Emirates was extraordinary.

First Champions League final in 20 years. First one where Arsenal genuinely have a team capable of winning it.

## The injury story nobody talks about

Arsenal won this with a diminished squad for large parts of the season. Jurrien Timber hasn't played since March. Ben White re-injured in May. Mikel Merino missed months with a foot injury sustained in January. At various points Arteta was rotating through defensive combinations that were not his first, second, or sometimes third choice.

Winning 85 points in the Premier League with those absences is a sign of depth. The squad has quality throughout it. Ben White at right back and Timber as the backup creates a situation where most clubs would consider their best player unavailable and Arsenal shrug and put out someone equally good.

This is what years of smart recruitment builds. Not just eleven starters. A squad.

## Nine clean sheets in the Champions League

Nine. In fourteen games. The defensive record in Europe has been even more extraordinary than the domestic one. Arsenal have conceded six goals in the entire Champions League campaign. Six. Most teams concede that in two games against top opposition.

The way they defend in Europe is different to how they defend in the league. In the Premier League they press higher, with more aggression, because they can. In the Champions League Arteta drops the block slightly, invites opponents to come onto them, and then hits on the transition. This tactical flexibility, the ability to play completely differently depending on the opponent and context, is the sign of a team that has genuinely internalised the system.

## What PSG represents

PSG are defending Champions League winners. They beat Real Madrid in the group stage. They beat Chelsea, Liverpool, and Bayern to reach the final. They are not a soft touch. Dembele and Kvaratskhelia on the wings is the best wide pair in the competition. Joao Neves is one of the best midfielders in Europe at 21. They have been ruthlessly efficient in the knockout rounds.

Arsenal beat them in the league phase when that mattered less. Finals are different. Finals are one game, 90 minutes, one mistake can end everything.

The thing Arsenal have that PSG might not is a collective identity that feels genuinely unbreakable. PSG have always had the talent. Whether they have the cohesion, the understanding that winning ugly is still winning, is the question. Their 5-4 first leg against Bayern was extraordinary going forward. It was also a defensive shambles that should have cost them the tie. Against Arsenal, you cannot defend like that.

## What Arteta has actually built

In 2021-22 Arsenal finished fifth and missed the Champions League. The following two seasons they finished second with 84 and 89 points respectively, close but not close enough. Each near-miss added to the narrative that they were nearly-men, a team that looked good but couldn't cross the line.

This season they crossed it. Emphatically.

The rebuild started with the defensive foundation, then the midfield structure, then the front four. Arteta added piece by piece, each summer addressing the exact weakness the previous season exposed. When the press wasn't coordinated enough, he worked on triggers. When they lacked a striker who could hold possession, he signed Gyokeres. When they needed Champions League nights to feel different, he adjusted the block.

This is not a manager who got lucky with a talented squad. This is a manager who has a clear idea of what he wants to build and has spent six years building it.

## My take

Arsenal are going to win the double.

I said Arsenal 2-1 in the preview I wrote two weeks ago and I'm sticking with it. The defensive record in Europe is too good to ignore. The collective identity is too strong. PSG have match-winners but Arsenal have a system that neutralises match-winners by removing the space they operate in.

The more interesting question, the one worth sitting with after Budapest, is what this means for the next five years. Arteta is getting a new contract. The core players are mostly 24-27 years old. Saliba, Saka, Odegaard, Gyokeres. This squad has not peaked.

The 2003-04 Invincibles are Arsenal's greatest achievement. This team is different, not worse. They won in a harder era, against deeper competition, with a squad held together by tactical intelligence and collective belief rather than individual brilliance.

That is, genuinely, harder to do.

Budapest on May 30. Set an alarm again.
