# World Cup 2026: Who Does the Data Say Wins?

**June 11, 2026**

It starts today. 48 teams, 3 host countries, 104 matches, and the most bloated group stage in football history. But strip all that away and the question is the same one it's always been: who actually lifts the trophy on July 19?

I went through the Opta supercomputer numbers, qualifying data, squad depth reports, and historical patterns to give you the cleanest read of who's genuinely in this and who's just making up the numbers.

---

## The Headline Numbers (Opta, 25,000 Simulations)

Opta ran their supercomputer through the full tournament 25,000 times. Here's where each contender lands on win probability:

| Spain | 16.0% | 52.1% | 39.0% | 25.6% |
| France | 12.8% | ~46% | ~33% | ~21% |
| England | 11.4% | 47.7% | 30.3% | 19.0% |
| Argentina | 10.4% | ~43% | ~28% | ~17% |
| Portugal | 6.8% | — | — | — |
| Brazil | 6.5% | — | — | — |
| Germany | 5.4% | — | — | — |
| Norway | 3.4% | — | — | — |

The first number that jumps out: Spain are the only team rated more likely than not (52.1%) to reach the quarter-finals. Every other team is a coin flip or worse just to get that far. That's not a small edge — that's a structural advantage baked into squad quality, group draw, and tactical coherence.

The second number: France, England, and Argentina are all clustered between 10-13%. This is genuinely the most wide-open World Cup in recent memory, which fits a tournament that just added 16 extra teams and scrambled nearly every bracket.

## Why Spain Is the Data Pick

The short version: Rodri is back, Pedri is healthy, and Yamal is Yamal.

Rodri missed almost the entire 2024-25 club season with a knee injury. He came back this season and was the first name on Luis de la Fuente's sheet the moment he was fit. The 2024 Ballon d'Or winner controls tempo, screens the back four, and is essentially Spain's heartbeat. Without him, Spain were beatable. With him, they have the best midfield floor of any team here.

Lamine Yamal had an absurd 2025-26 club season — 16 goals and 11 assists in La Liga, 6 goals and 4 assists in the Champions League at 18 years old — before a hamstring injury in late April cut his season short. He's in the squad and De la Fuente expects him ready for the June 15 opener against Cape Verde. There was a training scare on June 7 when Gavi caught him with a rough tackle and he walked off with a limp, but he finished the session. The story of this Spain team might come down to one question: is Yamal fully fit by the knockout rounds?

Even without him, Spain have Nico Williams, Dani Olmo, Pedri, and a defence that hasn't leaked easy goals in two years. They topped their qualifying group 75.3% of the time in Opta's simulations, which tells you how manageable Group H is.

## The England Case

England's qualifying campaign was statistically the cleanest of any team in UEFA — 8 wins from 8, 22 goals scored, **zero conceded**. That's not soft opponents either; they played Greece, Finland, Albania, Latvia, Andorra, and San Marino (fine, some of those help the numbers), but the pattern of complete control was consistent.

The 47.7% QF chance puts them level with Spain in terms of getting through the group and last 16. Where they fall off is in the knockout projections — 30.3% semi vs Spain's 39% — which suggests the data thinks they're capable of making deep runs but susceptible to one off night against a top side.

Jude Bellingham at Real Madrid had an extraordinary season, and the front three options give Southgate (or whoever ends up picking the team) genuine flexibility. The concern is the same one it's always been with England: set pieces and penalties defended under pressure, and whether Saka carries too much of the creative load in the final third.

---

## Argentina and the Defending Champion Curse

Argentina are the most interesting team to analyse because history is actively working against them.

Three of the last four defending champions went out in the group stage. Italy in 2010. Spain in 2014. Germany in 2018. The only exception was France in 2022, who made the final before losing to Argentina on penalties. And crucially: **no team has retained the World Cup since Brazil in 1962**. That's 64 years of failure from the defending champion.

The statistical weight behind this is real. Teams that win the World Cup often peak at the exact tournament, exhaust mentally and physically, and then get disrupted by tactical preparation (every coach in the world studies your patterns for four years). Argentina also have a specific issue: Messi is 38, and while he's still in the squad and targeting this tournament as his farewell, his club form has been managed carefully. He scored 8 goals in CONMEBOL qualifying — the most of any player — but that was spread across 18 matches and the physical load question is real.

Opta still gives them 10.4%, which respects the squad depth — Enzo Fernández, Rodrigo De Paul, Julián Álvarez, and a back four that concedes very little. But the history is a wall you can't ignore.

---

## France: The Quiet Favourite

At 12.8%, France are the slightly underrated pick in this list. Mbappé had his best club season yet: 25 goals and 5 assists in La Liga, 15 goals in 11 Champions League appearances at Real Madrid. He arrives at peak physical condition, without the contract drama and PSG politics that haunted the last three years of his career.

Didier Deschamps has a full squad — Tchouaméni and Camavinga in midfield, Saliba and Upamecano at the back, and enough depth in forward positions that injuries don't collapse the team. The one tactical question is whether France continue their low-block counter-attacking style (effective but joyless) or open up more with Mbappé in full flight. In major tournaments, Deschamps usually chooses boring and wins, which is the correct call.

If Mbappe hits form in the QF+ stages, France become the most dangerous team here. The gap between 12.8% and Spain's 16% is smaller than it looks on paper.

## The Dark Horse: Norway

Norway at 3.4% isn't the most exciting number until you remember what Erling Haaland did in qualifying. He led all of UEFA in shots (41), shots on target (28), expected goals (9.9 xG), and had 18 goal involvements across the campaign. He's 25 years old, fit, and the single most efficient finisher at this tournament.

The issue with Norway is everything around him. Their midfield is functional but not elite, their left side gets exposed against pressing teams, and the group draw hasn't been kind. But Haaland-based chaos is a real thing. One hot run in the knockout bracket and you're looking at a team that could beat anyone over 90 minutes.

Also worth noting: Portugal (6.8%) have Rúben Neves, Vitinha, and Pedro Neto alongside a squad that doesn't depend on Ronaldo the way they used to. He's still in the squad at 41 but the machinery no longer runs through him. That makes Portugal more tactically balanced — and more dangerous — than any of their recent World Cup campaigns.

---

## The Format Effect: 48 Teams Changes the Maths

This is the first 48-team World Cup, which means three-team groups and a last-48 round added to the bracket. The maths on this are important: stronger teams now have an extra match to grind through, which compounds injury risk for tournament favourites. It also means teams like Japan, Morocco, Senegal, and South Korea — all technically capable sides — have a legitimate path to the last 16 without needing to upset a major nation.

Across all qualifying campaigns globally, 2,527 goals were scored in 899 matches (2.81 goals per game average). That relatively open nature of qualifying doesn't always translate to the tournament itself, but the expanded format probably raises variance and makes group-stage upsets more likely.

---

## My Take

The data pick is Spain, and I think it's the right one. Rodri healthy and Yamal fit by the Round of 16 is a version of this team that's genuinely hard to stop. They have the best floor of any side — even on a bad day they control possession and don't give away easy goals — and when they click, they're the most complete team in the world right now.

France is the actual dark horse, not Norway. Mbappé's club form this season was something else, and if Deschamps trusts him in a more central attacking role (which he finally seems to be doing), this is the most lethal forward line in the tournament.

Argentina will make the quarters at minimum. Messi on a farewell run is unpredictable. But the history of defending champions is so damning I can't put them higher than third in my personal ranking.

England will bottle it somewhere between the semis and final. That's not pessimism, it's pattern recognition. They have the squad to go all the way, they just need to stop treating tournaments like a test they're not allowed to pass.

**Winner: Spain.** Runner-up: France. The upset team that goes further than expected: Morocco or Japan, somewhere in the bracket where the draw opens up.
