# The Daily Signal — Weekly Roundup
### March 30, 2026

---

Two big stories this week, and they couldn't be more different. One involves a 30-mile-wide strip of water in the Persian Gulf that just broke the stock market. The other involves a 19-year-old from Bologna who dropped to sixth on the opening lap at Suzuka and won by 13 seconds. Both are defining moments of 2026 so far. Let's get into it.

## 1. Wall Street Just Entered Correction Territory. The Word Nobody Wants to Say? Stagflation.

Five consecutive losing weeks. The Dow down 793 points in a single session. The Nasdaq in correction. The S&P 500 posting its worst month since September 2022. And the cause isn't a tech bubble popping or a bank collapsing — it's the Strait of Hormuz.

When Iran closed the strait on March 4, roughly 20% of the world's oil supply just... stopped. Brent crude went from $67 a barrel in late February to $126 at its peak. As of Friday, it's still trading north of $108. The Dallas Fed called this a "3-to-5x larger disruption" than both the 1973 Arab oil embargo and the 1979 Iranian Revolution. That's not hyperbole — those crises removed 4-6% of global supply. This one removed 20%.

**The numbers are ugly.** The Dow dropped 1.73% on Friday alone, officially entering correction territory. The S&P 500 is down 6.8% for March. The equal-weight version of the index — which strips out mega-cap distortion — is down 7% month-to-date. This isn't a narrow selloff in tech. Everything is bleeding.

Well, almost everything. Energy stocks are up 18.2% this month. Defense stocks up 14.7%. Gold just blew past $3,100. If your portfolio is Lockheed and ConocoPhillips, you're having a great month. Everyone else is watching red.

The sector that's getting destroyed? Consumer discretionary, down 12.3%. Airlines specifically: United down 22%, Delta 21%, American 24%. Jet fuel costs are up 31% since March 1, and Middle East route suspensions are killing high-margin international revenue. Earnings season is going to be brutal.

**Here's the part that should scare you.** The OECD raised its US inflation forecast for 2026 to 4.2% — nearly double the Fed's own projection. Surging energy costs are flowing into everything: transportation, logistics, food, fertilizer. And simultaneously, the economy is slowing. Consumer confidence is cratering. Recession fears are rising.

That's the textbook definition of stagflation. Rising prices plus falling growth.

The bond market sees it too. Futures traders pushed the probability of a Fed rate *hike* by year-end to 52%. At the start of 2026, three rate *cuts* were fully priced in. That's a complete 180 in four weeks.

The Fed's trapped. If oil-driven inflation keeps running hot, they have to raise rates — which makes the growth problem worse. You can't hike your way out of a closed shipping lane. Raising rates won't reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

President Trump paused energy plant strikes on March 27 for 10 days. Markets briefly rallied on the headline, then sold off again. Nobody trusts the timeline. The initial military operation was supposed to take 4 to 6 weeks. We're at week four, and the administration is already saying it "may extend past" that window.

**My read on this:** The single most telling stat is the rate hike probability crossing 50%. That number tells you the market's entire macro framework has shifted. The "soft landing achieved, rate cuts incoming" narrative? Dead. The new story is oil shock, stagflation risk, and a central bank with no good options.

What makes 2026 different from 2022 is the cause. In 2022, inflation was demand-driven — the Fed could crush it with rate hikes because the economy was overheating. This inflation is supply-driven. You can't tighten monetary policy to fix a geopolitical crisis.

The less obvious thing to watch: the AI infrastructure buildout. SoftBank just secured a $40 billion bridge loan for more OpenAI investment. The hyperscalers are still spending. But if the economy tips into actual stagflation, corporate AI budgets get scrutinized fast. The "spend now, figure out ROI later" era of AI investment cannot survive a recession.

Geopolitical crisis to energy crisis to market crisis. That's the chain. And the only way to break it is diplomatic — which nobody's confident about right now.

## 2. Antonelli Dropped to P6 on Lap 1 at Suzuka. He Won by 13 Seconds.

Three races into the 2026 F1 season and a 19-year-old from Bologna is leading the world championship. Not by a little. By a lot.

Kimi Antonelli had pole at Suzuka. Clean, comfortable pole. Then the lights went out and he got massive wheelspin off the line — the same energy harvesting gremlins that have bitten other teams all season finally caught Mercedes. He was P6 by Turn 1. Piastri swept into the lead. The McLaren finally looked like it belonged at the front.

For about thirty seconds, the narrative was "Antonelli finally cracked under pressure." The teenager made a mistake. The Suzuka fairy tale was dead.

It lasted four laps.

By Lap 5, Antonelli had already passed Alonso and Leclerc. By Lap 12, he was past Hamilton. Nothing reckless — he was just faster. The kind of faster where you don't need a late-braking lunge because you're alongside them before the braking zone starts. By Lap 18, he cleared his own teammate Russell. By Lap 28, he took Piastri through the Esses — not a divebomb, just more speed through the first apex.

From there? Gone. 13.7 seconds clear by the flag.

**The bigger story nobody's talking about enough:** Oliver Bearman lost the car at Turn 13 during the race. 50G impact. The Haas disintegrated. He walked away with a knee contusion — the halo and survival cell doing their job again. But the crash highlights a real safety concern with the 2026 regulations. The energy deployment system means cars can have wildly different speeds at the same point on the same straight depending on battery state. Bearman appeared to lose electrical deployment mid-corner at Suzuka speeds. That's terrifying.

The FIA has maybe two more races before something worse happens. Jeddah and Spa don't have the runoff margins to forgive a 50G hit.

**Meanwhile, Max Verstappen finished P8. Behind an Alpine.** The four-time world champion, at a track he's dominated in recent years, couldn't overtake Gasly. Red Bull are tied with Alpine on 16 points in the constructors'. Verstappen is ninth in the championship with 12 points. Antonelli has 72. That's a 60-point gap after three races. The performance clause conversations are probably already happening at Milton Keynes.

**Championship standings:** Antonelli 72, Russell 63, Leclerc 49, Hamilton 41... all the way down to Verstappen in P9 with 12.

**My read on this:** Three races, three data points, and the pattern is undeniable. This isn't a hot start propped up by a dominant car. Antonelli dropped to sixth on the opening lap — the kind of mistake that defines a teenager's race — and responded by systematically dismantling the entire field. He didn't panic. He just got to work.

That's the detail that separates him from "promising young driver" territory. Piastri, Hamilton, Leclerc, his own teammate — none of them had an answer once Antonelli was in clean air.

He replaced the greatest driver in F1 history, and after three races, nobody misses Hamilton at Mercedes. That's not shade at Lewis. That's the highest compliment you can pay the kid who took his seat.

Senna won his first championship at Suzuka. Schumacher clinched titles there. Verstappen sealed 2022 there. Antonelli just announced he belongs in that conversation. At nineteen.
