# Wall Street just entered correction territory. The word nobody wants to say? Stagflation.

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Topic:** Business

---

Five consecutive losing weeks. The Dow down 793 points in a single session. The Nasdaq in correction. The S&P 500 having its worst month since September 2022. And the thing that's causing all of it isn't a tech bubble popping or a bank collapsing — it's a 30-mile-wide strip of water between Iran and Oman.

The Strait of Hormuz crisis has officially broken the market's back.

## The numbers are ugly

On Friday March 27, the Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 793.47 points — 1.73% — to close at 45,166.64, officially entering correction territory (more than 10% off its all-time high). The S&P 500 lost 1.67%, ending at 6,368.85 — now down 6.8% for the month. The Nasdaq fell 2.15% to 20,948.36 and had already entered correction territory the day before.

The S&P 500 hasn't had a month this bad since March 2025. The equal-weight version of the index (which strips out mega-cap distortion) is down 7% month-to-date — worst since September 2022. This isn't a narrow selloff in tech. Everything is bleeding.

Well, almost everything. Energy stocks are up 18.2% this month. Defense stocks are up 14.7%. Gold miners are rallying because gold just smashed past $3,100 an ounce. If your portfolio is Lockheed Martin and ConocoPhillips, congratulations. Everyone else is watching red.

Consumer discretionary is the worst-performing sector, down 12.3%. Airlines specifically are in freefall — United down 22%, Delta down 21%, American down 24%. Jet fuel costs are up 31% since March 1, and Middle East route suspensions are wiping out high-margin international revenue. This isn't priced in yet. Earnings season is going to be brutal.

## How a strait broke the global economy

So how did we get here? The US and Israel launched military operations against Iran in late February. Iran responded by closing the Strait of Hormuz on March 4 — the 30-mile chokepoint that handles roughly 20% of the world's oil supply.

Iran's IRGC wasn't subtle about it. Their messaging: "Not a litre of oil" will pass through.

The impact was immediate. Oil production from Kuwait, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE collectively dropped by 6.7 million barrels per day by March 10, and by at least 10 million by March 12. Brent crude went from $67 per barrel on February 27 to $126 at its peak. WTI crossed $111. As of Friday, Brent is trading between $108 and $114.

The Dallas Fed published research on March 20 modeling three scenarios. In the best case — a one-quarter closure — WTI averages $98 per barrel in Q2 and global real GDP growth takes a 2.9 percentage point hit on an annualized basis. In the worst case, three quarters of closure, oil hits $132 by year-end and global GDP growth drops by 1.3 percentage points for the full year.

For context, the Dallas Fed called this a "3-to-5x larger disruption" than both the 1973 Arab oil embargo (which removed 6% of supply) and the 1979 Iranian Revolution (4%). Twenty percent of global supply just... stopped. About 80% of that oil was headed to Asia.

## The S-word

This is where it gets uncomfortable. The OECD raised its US inflation forecast for 2026 to 4.2% — nearly double the Fed's own projection of 2.7%. Surging energy costs are flowing through to everything: transportation, logistics, food production, fertilizer imports. Consumer staples are down 4.1% this month because companies can't absorb the input cost increases.

And on the other side of the equation, the economy is slowing. Consumer confidence is cratering. Recession fears are rising. The textbook definition of stagflation — rising prices plus falling growth — is staring us in the face.

The bond market is screaming it too. Friday morning, futures traders pushed the probability of a Fed rate *hike* by year-end to 52%. Let that sink in. At the start of 2026, three rate *cuts* were fully priced in. We've gone from expecting monetary easing to pricing in tightening. That's a 180-degree shift in four weeks.

The Fed held rates steady at its March meeting, but the message was clear: if oil-driven inflation keeps running hot, they'll have to respond. And the only tool they have is raising rates — which would make the growth problem worse. It's the classic stagflation trap, and the Fed knows it.

## The Trump factor

President Trump paused energy plant destruction strikes on March 27 for 10 days, stating "talks are ongoing." Secretary of State Rubio told G7 ministers that "another 2 to 4 weeks" are needed to wrap up operations and insisted ground troops aren't necessary.

Markets briefly rallied on the pause headline before selling off again. Wall Street doesn't trust the timeline. The initial military operation was supposed to take 4 to 6 weeks. We're now at week four, and the US is acknowledging it "may extend past" that window.

Meanwhile, the partial government shutdown means TSA officers have been working without pay for weeks. Paychecks are supposedly coming March 30. The optics of a government that can't pay its airport security workers while conducting a Middle East military operation are... not great for consumer sentiment.

## What happens next

The Dallas Fed's modeling suggests that if the supply disruption gets reduced from 20% to 10% — through reopening shipping lanes, SPR releases, or OPEC+ adjustments — the GDP hit drops from -2.9 to about -1.6 percentage points. Significant, but manageable.

But if Hormuz stays closed through Q3? Oil at $115. Global growth crushed. And a Fed that has to choose between fighting inflation and preventing a recession — which is the definition of a no-win scenario for monetary policy.

The 10-day pause on strikes is the market's best hope right now. If it leads to actual negotiations and an eventual reopening of the strait, this correction is a buying opportunity. If it's just a pause before escalation, we're looking at a much deeper drawdown.

## My take

I keep coming back to one stat: the probability of a rate hike crossing 50% for the first time. That's the number that tells you the market's entire macro framework has shifted. At the start of 2026, the story was "soft landing achieved, rate cuts incoming, tech bull market continues." That narrative is dead. The new narrative is "oil shock, stagflation risk, and a Fed with no good options."

What makes this different from the 2022 selloff is the cause. In 2022, inflation was demand-driven — the Fed could (and did) crush it with rate hikes because the economy was overheating. This inflation is supply-driven. You can't hike your way out of a closed shipping lane. Raising rates won't reopen the Strait of Hormuz. All it does is slow down an economy that's already decelerating.

The energy and defense trade feels obvious in hindsight, but I think the less obvious play is watching what happens to the AI infrastructure buildout. SoftBank just secured a $40 billion bridge loan for more OpenAI investment and data centers. The hyperscalers are still spending. But if the economy actually tips into stagflation, corporate AI budgets are going to get scrutinized hard and fast. The "spend now, figure out ROI later" era of AI investment can't survive a recession.

Geopolitical crisis → energy crisis → market crisis. That's the chain. And the only way to break it is diplomatic — which, right now, nobody's confident about.

Five straight weeks of red. The Dow in correction. The S-word hanging over everything. Good luck out there.
