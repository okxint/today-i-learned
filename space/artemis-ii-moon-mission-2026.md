# Artemis II just launched. Humans are going back to the moon. And the toilet broke on day one.

**Date:** 2026-04-02
**Topic:** Space / Geopolitics

---

Four astronauts are currently hurtling through space toward the moon right now. First time humans have left low Earth orbit since 1972. Fifty-four years. More than half a century of looking up and not going back. And then on April 1st — April Fools' Day, because NASA apparently has a sense of humor — they finally did it.

The SLS rocket launched from Kennedy Space Center at 6:35 PM EDT. The crew: Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, and Mission Specialists Christina Koch and Jeremy Hansen. If those names don't ring a bell, here's why they should. Glover is the first Black person to leave low Earth orbit. Koch is the first woman. Hansen is the first non-American. Fifty-four years of "we'll get back to it eventually," and the crew that finally goes is the most diverse in spaceflight history. That's not a diversity checkbox — that's what happens when you stop sending the same twelve guys.

## What they're actually doing up there

Artemis II is a ten-day flyby mission. They're not landing. They're looping around the moon on a free-return trajectory — basically a slingshot that uses the moon's gravity to whip the spacecraft back toward Earth. Think of it as the universe's most expensive test drive.

Day 1 was all about orbit setup and proximity operations. Victor Glover spent about an hour manually flying the Orion capsule around the spent upper stage of the SLS rocket. Manually. As in, hands on the controls, steering a spacecraft with reaction control thrusters while the Earth spins below. That's not something you practice in a simulator and assume it works. NASA needed to prove a human can actually do it.

Day 6 is the big one. The crew will pass within 4,100 miles of the moon's far side — the part no human has ever seen with their own eyes in real time. They'll be out of radio contact with Earth. Just four people, alone, on the other side of the moon. Taking photos. Making observations. Experiencing something that literally nobody alive has ever experienced.

Day 10: splashdown in the Pacific.

## The toilet situation

Because no historic achievement is complete without a plumbing issue, the Orion capsule's toilet broke within hours of launch. Christina Koch flagged a malfunction in the Universal Waste Management System — NASA's extremely dignified name for the space toilet. Specifically, the urine collection subsystem had a faulty controller.

The crew and mission control in Houston troubleshot it together and got it working. But imagine: you're on the most important space mission in half a century, further from Earth than any human has been since Nixon was president, and your first crisis is that nobody can pee. Peak humanity right there.

## $93 billion and counting

Let's talk about the elephant in the room. The Artemis program has cost roughly $93 billion over 13 years, according to NASA's Inspector General. The SLS rocket alone slipped its first launch date more than 26 times. Original target: December 2016. Actual first launch (Artemis I, uncrewed): November 2022. Six years late. Every month of delay means continued workforce costs, facility maintenance, and hardware sitting in hangars aging.

The Trump administration's own fiscal year 2026 budget described SLS as "grossly expensive" and flagged it at 140% over its original budget. A single Artemis mission costs about $4 billion to operate. For comparison, SpaceX launches a Falcon 9 for around $67 million. That's a 60x cost difference.

Critics are loud and they're not wrong on the numbers. Reason Magazine ran a piece calling it "a monument to government waste." New Space Economy asked whether the money could be better spent on Earth's crises. A third of Bank of America's fund managers already think the government overspends on projects like this.

## But here's the thing nobody talks about

NASA's entire annual budget is $25 billion. That's less than 0.5% of federal spending. Americans spend more on pizza. The Department of Defense budget is north of $900 billion. The entire Artemis program — spread over 13 years — costs roughly what the Pentagon spends in five weeks.

And Artemis II isn't just a joyride. It's a systems validation mission. NASA is testing Orion's life support, navigation, communication, and thermal protection systems with actual humans on board. If this works, it paves the way for Artemis III (a crewed mission testing rendezvous procedures), then Artemis IV in 2028 — which could be the first lunar landing since Apollo 17. After that, the plan is yearly landings building toward a permanent lunar base.

The geopolitical angle is massive too. China's lunar program is accelerating. They've landed on the far side of the moon, returned samples, and announced plans for a crewed lunar mission by 2030 and a permanent base by 2035. India's Chandrayaan program is active. Russia and China signed a joint agreement for an International Lunar Research Station. The moon isn't just science anymore — it's strategic territory. Whoever builds permanent infrastructure there first gets a say in the rules.

NASA administrator Jared Isaacman knows this. The revised mission plan pushes a landing to Artemis IV specifically because they want to test both SpaceX's Starship HLS and Blue Origin's lander in low Earth orbit first. They're not rushing it. After $93 billion and 26 schedule slips, the last thing they need is a failure on the one mission everyone's watching.

## My take

There's something that hit me watching the launch footage. The SLS is absurdly inefficient. It's a cost-plus contractor's dream and a taxpayer's nightmare. The schedule overruns are embarrassing. The per-mission cost is indefensible when SpaceX exists.

And none of that mattered the moment it left the pad.

Four people — representing three countries, multiple backgrounds, half the human population that's never had anyone in deep space before — are currently floating between Earth and the moon. Victor Glover manually flew a spacecraft around a rocket stage while orbiting Earth. Christina Koch diagnosed a broken toilet using a procedure beamed up from Houston. Jeremy Hansen is the first Canadian to leave Earth orbit. Reid Wiseman is commanding a ship that no one had ever flown with crew before.

$93 billion is a lot of money. It's also roughly what Americans spend on lottery tickets every four years. We're fine.

The real question isn't whether Artemis is worth it. It's whether we're serious about what comes next. China isn't debating whether lunar exploration is cost-effective. They're building it. India isn't writing op-eds about whether Chandrayaan's budget could be better spent elsewhere. They're landing on the moon.

The SLS is probably the last of its kind — a Senate-designed rocket built to preserve jobs in specific congressional districts. Starship will eventually replace it and do the same job for a fraction of the cost. But Starship wasn't ready. SLS was. And right now, four people are seeing the far side of the moon because of it.

Sometimes you don't need the most efficient path. You just need one that gets you there.
