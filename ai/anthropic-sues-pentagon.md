# Anthropic Sued the Pentagon — And It Might Be the Most Important AI Story of the Year

**March 11, 2026**

Anthropic, the company behind Claude, filed two federal lawsuits against the Department of Defense on March 9 after being designated a "supply chain risk" — a label historically reserved for Chinese firms like Huawei and ZTE. As the Council on Foreign Relations put it: "No Chinese AI firm has been designated a supply chain risk by the U.S. government. Only Anthropic enjoys that distinction."

## How We Got Here

Last July, the Pentagon awarded Anthropic a ~$200M contract. Claude became the first frontier AI model approved for classified government networks — used in targeting operations and intelligence work, including operations against Iran.

Then the Pentagon shifted. In January 2026, a new AI Strategy memo directed all defense AI contracts to include an "any lawful use" clause within 180 days. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth wanted unrestricted access to Claude for any military purpose.

Anthropic drew exactly two red lines:
1. No fully autonomous weapons without human decision-making in targeting and firing
2. No mass surveillance of American citizens

That's it. They offered to support virtually everything else — including missile defense — at nominal cost.

Hegseth gave CEO Dario Amodei an ultimatum: agree by 5:01 PM on February 27, or face consequences. Anthropic refused. Within hours, Trump directed all federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's products, and OpenAI swooped in to secure its own Pentagon deal — apparently agreeing to terms Anthropic had rejected.

## The Legal Battle

Anthropic filed in two courts. In California, the five-count complaint argues: the Pentagon bypassed legally required procedures, the designation constitutes First Amendment retaliation for Anthropic's AI safety stance, and the government violated due process. The complaint states: "The Constitution does not allow the government to wield its enormous power to punish a company for its protected speech."

In D.C., a separate petition challenges the supply chain risk determination under its own statutory framework.

The legal analysis is damning for the Pentagon. As attorney Zach Prince noted, the government is simultaneously claiming Anthropic's AI is critical for national security (threatening to invoke the Defense Production Act to force cooperation) while also calling the same company a supply chain risk. "This is obviously nothing to do with national security interests."

## The Fallout Is Massive

The "supply chain risk" label doesn't just ban Anthropic from government contracts — it cascades. Every defense contractor must now certify they don't use Claude in any Pentagon work. Anthropic's CFO estimates this could reduce 2026 revenue by "multiple billions of dollars" — potentially cutting defense-exposed revenue by 50-100%. The company is valued at $380B with $14B in run-rate revenue.

But the industry response was unexpected. Over 30 employees from OpenAI and Google DeepMind filed an amicus brief supporting Anthropic. Nearly 900 Google and OpenAI staff signed an open letter backing Anthropic's red lines. Google's chief scientist Jeff Dean personally signed the brief — even as Google itself deepened its own Pentagon AI relationship the day after Anthropic sued.

OpenAI's position has been messy. CEO Sam Altman claimed their Pentagon deal includes similar safeguards, but refused to release the contract text. Former Army Under Secretary Brad Carson said bluntly: "I don't believe that provision is in the contract." The Intercept reported OpenAI's surveillance clause only prohibits "intentional" domestic surveillance — leaving a loophole for "incidental collection." OpenAI later admitted the announcement looked "sloppy and opportunistic."

## The Leaked Memo That Made Everything Worse

On March 5, an internal Amodei memo leaked. In it, he said Trump disliked Anthropic for not giving him "dictator-style praise," called OpenAI's Pentagon deal "safety theater," accused Sam Altman of "spin/gaslighting," and called OpenAI staff "gullible." He apologized publicly, calling it an "out-of-date assessment" written on a difficult day. The damage was done.

## Why This Actually Matters

This isn't a contract dispute. It's a constitutional test case that will define the AI industry's relationship with government power for the next decade.

The precedent being set: Can the government economically destroy a company for refusing to remove safety restrictions from its technology? The supply chain risk statute was designed to protect against foreign adversary sabotage — using it against an American company for a policy disagreement has no precedent.

Legal experts largely predict Anthropic will win a preliminary injunction. But the bigger question is what Congress does next. As experts across the spectrum agree, a company's terms of service shouldn't define the boundaries of military AI use — that's Congress's job. But Congress hasn't acted, creating a governance vacuum that this lawsuit now fills.

The Google Project Maven parallel from 2018 keeps getting cited, but the stakes are incomparably higher. AI is now embedded in active military operations, not theoretical drone surveillance. The outcome will determine whether AI companies can maintain safety positions when doing business with the world's largest customer.

**My take:** Anthropic's two red lines — no autonomous killing without human oversight, no mass surveillance of Americans — are genuinely reasonable. If these are controversial enough to get you blacklisted, the government is telling the entire AI industry that safety restrictions are a liability, not a feature. The irony is that Anthropic was willing to do almost everything the Pentagon wanted. They just wanted a human in the loop for lethal decisions. The fact that this was a dealbreaker says more about the Pentagon's ambitions than about Anthropic's restrictions. Watch whether the preliminary injunction holds — if it does, this becomes the landmark case that forces Congress to finally legislate AI governance.
