# $690 billion on AI infrastructure in one year. Even the people spending it are calling it a bubble.

**Date:** 2026-03-31
**Topic:** AI

---

Five companies are about to spend $690 billion on AI infrastructure in 2026. That's more than the GDP of Sweden. More than the entire Apollo program adjusted for inflation. More than every railroad ever laid in America, combined.

And the guy who runs one of those companies — Jeff Bezos — called it "kind of an industrial bubble." Sam Altman said "people will overinvest and lose money." Jamie Dimon said there's a higher chance of a "meaningful stock drop" than the market reflects.

These are not bears on CNBC. These are the people writing the checks. And they're still writing them.

## The numbers that broke my brain

Let's start with what the hyperscalers are actually committing:

| Company | 2026 Projected Capex |
|---------|---------------------|
| Amazon | ~$200B |
| Alphabet | $175-185B |
| Microsoft | $120B+ |
| Meta | $115-135B |
| Oracle | ~$50B |
| **Total** | **$660-690B** |

For context, these same five companies spent $256 billion in 2024. Then $443 billion in 2025 — a 57% jump. Now they're projecting another 36% increase. About 75% of this is going directly to AI infrastructure. That's roughly $450 billion dedicated to GPUs, data centers, and cooling systems in a single year.

Goldman Sachs projects total hyperscaler capex from 2025-2027 will reach $1.15 trillion. To finance the 2025 portion alone, these companies raised $108 billion in debt. The projection for the full buildout? $1.5 trillion in debt issuance.

Jensen Huang is smiling somewhere.

## The week that crystallized everything

Last week alone tells the whole story. In the span of five days:

**Mistral AI secured $830 million in debt financing** — not equity, debt — to build its first proprietary data center near Paris. A consortium of seven banks led by Bpifrance (France's state investment bank) is funding 13,800 Nvidia GB300 GPUs with 44 megawatts of capacity. Mistral has now raised over $3.8 billion total. This is the first major European AI lab to build its own data center. It's a sovereignty play disguised as a business decision.

**Starcloud raised $170 million at a $1.1 billion valuation** — becoming the fastest YC startup ever to hit unicorn status, 17 months after demo day. Their pitch: put data centers in space. Not a metaphor. They literally launched an H100 GPU into orbit on a Falcon 9 last November. Next up: a 200kW, 3-ton spacecraft designed for SpaceX Starship's deployment system. The logic is that AI compute demand is outpacing Earth's power grid, land availability, and cooling constraints. Space has vacuum cooling, 24/7 solar, and zero NIMBYism. Wild? Sure. But Benchmark and EQT Ventures just wrote a check for $170 million, so someone's buying it.

**Shield AI closed a $2 billion mega-round at a $12.7 billion valuation** — led by Advent International and JPMorgan, with $500 million in preferred equity from Blackstone on top. Their Hivemind autonomous flight software got selected by the U.S. Air Force for next-gen combat aircraft. Their V-BAT drones are actively deployed in Ukraine. Projecting $540 million in revenue for 2026 with 80%+ growth. Defense AI isn't a future bet — it's generating revenue today, in actual warzones.

And that's before you get to robotics.

## Robotics is having its moment

In one week in March, robotics companies raised over $1.2 billion combined:

- **Mind Robotics** — $500M Series A (Accel + a16z), industrial AI robots spun out of Rivian
- **Rhoda AI** — $450M Series A at $1.7B valuation (Premji Invest), training robot foundation models on internet videos
- **Sunday** — $165M Series B at $1.15B valuation (Coatue), household humanoid robot called "Memo"

And that's the *small* rounds. Skild AI raised $1.4 billion in January at a $14 billion valuation, led by SoftBank. Figure AI closed over $1 billion at a $39 billion valuation last September. Plans to ship 100,000 humanoid robots over four years.

2026 is on pace for $20 billion+ in robotics funding. The thesis across the board is the same: LLMs taught software to understand language; the next step is teaching hardware to understand the physical world. Whether that happens in 2026 or 2036 is the trillion-dollar question.

## Then there's OpenAI

OpenAI hit $25 billion in annualized revenue as of February 2026. For perspective:

- 2023: ~$2B
- 2024: ~$6B
- 2025: $13.1B
- Feb 2026: $25B annualized, projecting $30B for the full year and $62B for 2027

From near-zero to $25 billion ARR in 39 months. No enterprise software company has ever scaled this fast. Not Salesforce. Not Snowflake. Nobody.

They're now valued at roughly $730 billion privately and reportedly targeting up to $1 trillion for an IPO filing in late 2026 or early 2027. If that happens, it would be the largest public offering in history.

Meanwhile, OpenAI committed $300 billion to Oracle over five years. They generated $13 billion in revenue in 2025. That commitment is 23x their annual revenue. The conviction — or the recklessness, depending on your perspective — is staggering.

## The 20:1 problem

Here's where the bears have a point.

Total AI revenue across the entire industry in 2025: less than $50 billion. Total investment in AI infrastructure: over $1 trillion. That's a 20-to-1 investment-to-revenue ratio.

Bank of America's February fund manager survey found that nearly a third of respondents believe corporations are overinvesting in AI — the highest reading on record, going back to 2005. Forty percent of CEOs surveyed agree. MIT research found that 95% of organizations achieved zero ROI on $30-40 billion in GenAI spending across 300+ initiatives.

Time Magazine ran a piece last week titled "We Must Prepare for an AI Bubble Now," comparing the current dynamic not to the dot-com bust but to the 2008 financial crisis. Yale published an analysis outlining three burst scenarios: concentration leading to contagion (too few companies controlling too many deals), governance failures (a malfunctioning public AI model damaging financial markets), and technological obsolescence (quantum breakthroughs rendering billions in GPU infrastructure worthless overnight).

Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon put it plainly: he expects "a lot of capital deployed that doesn't deliver returns."

## My take

I keep going back to one number: 20-to-1. A trillion dollars invested against less than $50 billion in revenue. That ratio screams bubble. The dot-com era had a comparable ratio right before it popped.

But here's what's different. The dot-com bubble was built on Pets.com and Webvan — companies with no real utility. AI infrastructure is being deployed by companies that already generate trillions in revenue and have balance sheets that can absorb a write-down. Amazon isn't going bankrupt if its $200 billion AI bet takes longer to pay off. It's not going to be fun for shareholders, but it's not existential.

The real risk isn't that AI doesn't work. It clearly works. OpenAI's revenue trajectory proves demand is real. The risk is that the infrastructure buildout overshoots demand by such a massive margin that the correction, when it comes, takes everything else down with it. $1.5 trillion in debt issuance means this isn't just a tech sector problem — it's baked into the bond market, the banking system, and pension funds.

Starcloud is putting GPUs in orbit. Shield AI is flying autonomous drones in Ukraine. Mistral is building a French national AI data center backed by state banks. The money isn't going to PowerPoint decks and vaporware — it's going to physical infrastructure. Racks. Chips. Satellites. Drones.

The question isn't whether AI infrastructure spending is a bubble. By any historical measure, it is. The question is whether this is the kind of bubble where the infrastructure outlives the mania — like railroads, like fiber optic cables, like the internet itself — or the kind where the debt load brings the whole house down.

$690 billion in one year. Even the people spending it think some of it will be wasted. They're spending it anyway. That's either the most rational thing that's ever happened in capitalism or the most irrational. Probably both.
