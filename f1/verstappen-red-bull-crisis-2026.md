# Max Verstappen is stuck in a car he hates, and Red Bull doesn't have an answer

**Date:** 2026-03-27
**Topic:** F1

---

Two races into the 2026 season and the four-time world champion has 12 points. Twelve. Kimi Antonelli — the teenager who was crashing in FP1 sessions 18 months ago — has 47.

Red Bull's new era is off to a catastrophic start, and Max Verstappen isn't hiding his frustration. He called the RB22 "incredibly tough to drive" and compared the new-gen F1 cars to "playing Mario Kart." That's not banter. That's a driver who's genuinely miserable.

## What's actually wrong with the RB22?

The rear end is a mess. Verstappen is making constant steering corrections just to keep the car pointing forward, especially in high-speed corners. The car that Adrian Newey built to dance through Suzuka's S-curves? Yeah, that DNA is gone.

But it's worse than just handling. There's a fundamental issue with energy harvesting on formation laps. The new 2026 power units require drivers to harvest enough electrical energy before the start to get proper acceleration off the line. Red Bull hasn't figured this out. In China's sprint, Verstappen dropped from P8 to P15 on the opening lap. Not because he braked late or got squeezed — he literally didn't have the energy to accelerate.

And then his power unit died entirely in the Chinese GP. Coolant fault. DNF. Verstappen's best result from two weekends? Ninth in Australia. The reigning world champion finished ninth.

## The Toto Wolff quote that stings

Toto Wolff — ever the diplomat — called Verstappen's start to 2026 a "horror show." When your rival team boss is using phrases like that, you know it's bad. And he can afford to be generous — his team just delivered back-to-back 1-2 finishes.

## Meanwhile, Haas is ahead of Red Bull

Read that again. **Haas is P4 in the constructors'. Red Bull is behind them.**

Oliver Bearman — 20 years old, in a Haas — is fifth in the drivers' championship with 17 points. He's outscoring Verstappen by five points. Bearman's been quietly brilliant: P7 in Australia, P5 in China, and he's the one saying Haas has a "great baseline" in race pace. The kid is not even slightly intimidated.

Haas beating Red Bull on merit. That's not a meme. That's the 2026 standings after two rounds.

## The bigger picture: new regs always create chaos

Every major regulation change reshuffles the grid. It happened in 2014 when Mercedes figured out the turbo-hybrid formula before everyone else and dominated for years. It happened in 2022 when Red Bull nailed ground effect and won back-to-back-to-back titles.

The 2026 rules are the biggest shake-up in F1 history. Active aerodynamics replacing DRS. Front and rear wings that physically change shape mid-corner. A completely new power unit formula with a 50/50 split between combustion and electrical power. These aren't tweaks — the sport basically designed a new category.

Mercedes clearly understood the assignment. Two 1-2 finishes. Russell leads the championship with 51 points. Antonelli won in Shanghai with pole, fastest lap, and the race win — becoming the second-youngest winner in F1 history behind Verstappen himself. The irony writes itself.

Ferrari isn't far behind either. Hamilton got his first Ferrari podium in China and says the SF-26 feels like "a go-kart" — the highest compliment he gives any car. Both Hamilton and Leclerc are in the top four. Even Audi scored points on debut through Bortoleto in Australia.

Red Bull? They're firefighting. And Suzuka — a high-speed circuit that will punish rear instability — is this weekend.

## The exit clause elephant in the room

Everyone knows Verstappen has performance-related exit clauses in his Red Bull contract. If the car doesn't improve, the conversation shifts from "when will Red Bull catch up?" to "where is Max going in 2027?" Mercedes has two young guns performing at an elite level. Ferrari has Hamilton and Leclerc locked in. McLaren is fast but inconsistent.

The silly season might start earlier than anyone expected this year.

## My take

Verstappen in a bad car is still one of the five best drivers on the grid. But he's not a miracle worker, and the RB22 needs fundamental fixes — not upgrades, fixes. The energy harvesting issue on starts is costing him positions before he even gets to Turn 1, and the rear instability means he's spending cognitive load just surviving rather than racing.

The thing that worries me most for Red Bull isn't the pace gap. Regulation changes always create gaps, and those close over time. What worries me is Verstappen's tone. He's not doing the usual "we'll work hard and come back stronger" routine. He's openly trashing the car concept and the regulations themselves. That's a driver who's checking out mentally.

If Suzuka goes badly — and on paper, a high-downforce track with fast corners should expose the RB22's weaknesses even further — the pressure on Christian Horner will be immense. And Verstappen's patience has a limit.

Two races in, and 2026 is already the most unpredictable F1 season in a decade. Red Bull went from untouchable to uncompetitive in one regulation change. That's the sport. But watching it happen to a generational talent like Verstappen in real time? That's painful.
