# Antonelli dropped to P6 on Lap 1 at Suzuka. He won by 13 seconds.

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Topic:** F1

---

I said to watch Suzuka. I said if Antonelli wins there, at 19, at the track where Senna became a legend, the hype would be fully justified.

He didn't just win. He put on a masterclass in race management that most veterans couldn't pull off. And he did it the hard way — from sixth place.

## The Start That Should've Ended His Race

Antonelli had pole. Comfortable, clean pole. And then the lights went out and... wheelspin. Massive, ugly wheelspin off the line. The same energy harvesting issues that have plagued other teams all season bit Mercedes for the first time. He dropped like a stone — P6 by Turn 1. Piastri, who'd been lurking on the front row, swept into the lead. Russell slotted into second. The McLaren finally looked like it belonged at the front.

For about thirty seconds, the narrative was "Antonelli finally cracked under pressure." The teenager made a mistake. The Suzuka fairy tale was dead.

It lasted exactly four laps.

## The Recovery

By Lap 5, Antonelli had already dispatched Alonso and Leclerc. By Lap 12, he was past Hamilton. Clean, clinical overtakes — nothing reckless, nothing desperate. He was just faster. Significantly faster. The kind of faster where you don't even need a late-braking lunge because you're alongside them before the braking zone even starts.

By Lap 18, he was on Russell's gearbox. His teammate, who'd been frustrated all weekend by a setup error in qualifying, had no answer. Antonelli breezed past on the back straight and set about hunting Piastri.

And then Lap 22 happened.

## The Bearman Crash

Oliver Bearman lost the car at Turn 13 — the fast left-hander before the chicane. Fifty Gs. The Haas disintegrated on impact. Safety car, immediate red flag concerns, the whole paddock holding its breath.

He walked away. Knee contusion. That's it. The halo and the survival cell did their job again.

But here's what matters beyond the relief: Bearman's crash is exactly the scenario Norris warned about after Melbourne. The speed differentials created by the new energy regulations mean that when a car is harvesting and another is deploying, you get closing speeds that are genuinely dangerous. Bearman appeared to lose electrical deployment mid-corner. At Suzuka speeds, through Turn 13, that's a death trap.

The FIA needs to address this before we get to Jeddah or Spa. A 50G impact at a circuit with better runoff is scary. A 50G impact at a circuit without it could be catastrophic.

## Safety Car Chaos

The safety car bunched the field and absolutely destroyed Piastri's strategy. He'd built a 4.5-second gap over Russell and was managing his tires perfectly. The restart put Antonelli — on fresher rubber after a perfectly timed pit stop under the safety car — right behind him.

Piastri held on for three laps. Three brave, committed laps around Suzuka with a Mercedes filling his mirrors. On Lap 28, Antonelli got him through the Esses. Not a divebomb, not a desperate move — he just carried more speed through the first apex and Piastri had nowhere to go.

From there? Gone. Antonelli pulled 13.7 seconds in the remaining laps. Piastri held second. Russell salvaged third after his qualifying woes.

## And Then There's Verstappen

P8. Behind Gasly. In an Alpine.

I wrote two days ago that Suzuka would expose the RB22's weaknesses, and that's exactly what happened. Verstappen spent most of the race stuck behind Gasly with zero ability to overtake. The four-time world champion, at a track he's dominated in recent years, couldn't get past a midfield Alpine.

Red Bull are tied with Alpine in the constructors' on 16 points. That sentence still doesn't feel real. Verstappen is ninth in the championship with 12 points. Antonelli has 72. The gap is 60 points after three races.

## The Standings

**Drivers':** Antonelli 72, Russell 63, Leclerc 49, Hamilton 41... all the way down to Verstappen P9 with 12.

**Constructors':** Mercedes 135, then daylight, then everyone else. Red Bull tied with Alpine at 16.

Antonelli is now the youngest-ever championship leader in Formula 1 history. Nineteen years old. Leading the world championship after the third round. Ahead of his more experienced teammate. Ahead of every former world champion on the grid.

## My take

Three races, three data points, and now the pattern is undeniable. This isn't a hot start that'll cool off. This isn't a car carrying a young driver to flattering results. Antonelli dropped to sixth on the opening lap at Suzuka — the kind of mistake that defines a teenager's Sunday — and responded by carving through the field like he'd done it a hundred times.

That's the detail that separates Antonelli from "promising young driver" territory. He doesn't panic. He dropped six places and his engineer probably didn't even hear a change in his breathing on the radio. He just got to work. Piastri, Hamilton, Leclerc, his own teammate — none of them had an answer once Antonelli was in clean air with a car underneath him.

The Red Bull crisis I wrote about? It's worse than I thought. I expected Suzuka to be painful for Verstappen. I didn't expect him to be physically unable to overtake an Alpine. The performance clause conversations are probably already happening behind closed doors at Milton Keynes.

And the Bearman crash raises questions that go beyond one incident. The 2026 regulations created a formula where cars can have radically different speeds at the same point on the same straight depending on battery state. That's an engineering feature that's also a safety flaw. The FIA's got maybe two more races before something worse happens.

But the headline is Antonelli. The kid from Bologna who replaced Lewis Hamilton and made everyone forget about Lewis Hamilton. Three races in, 72 points, championship leader, youngest ever. Senna won his first championship at Suzuka. Schumacher clinched titles there. Verstappen sealed 2022 there in controversial fashion.

Antonelli just announced that he belongs in that conversation. At nineteen.

The trilogy is complete. Mercedes are dominant. Red Bull are lost. And the future of Formula 1 has a name, and it's Andrea Kimi Antonelli.
